Anatomy of Pharynx

Q21. Regarding to pharynx , all true except :

  • A. Include Nasopharynx, oropharynx, Hypopharynx
  • B. Differ tissue from region to region
  • C. Start from base of skull to level of 5th cervical vertebrae
  • D. Palatine tonsil is point of oropharynx

Correct answer: C
a. (True) The pharynx is anatomically divided into three parts: the nasopharynx (superior, posterior to the nasal cavity), the oropharynx (middle, posterior to the oral cavity), and the hypopharynx or laryngopharynx (inferior, posterior to the larynx).
b. (True) The epithelial lining of the pharynx varies by region. The nasopharynx is predominantly lined by respiratory epithelium (pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium), while the oropharynx and hypopharynx, which are also part of the digestive tract and subject to abrasion from food, are lined by non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium.
c. (False) The pharynx extends from the base of the skull (basiocciput and basisphenoid) superiorly down to the inferior border of the cricoid cartilage, which corresponds to the level of the C6 (sixth) cervical vertebra, where it becomes continuous with the esophagus. Therefore, stating it extends to the level of the 5th cervical vertebra is incorrect. This is the "except" statement.
d. (True) The palatine tonsils are prominent lymphoid tissues located in the lateral walls of the oropharynx, specifically within the tonsillar fossa between the palatoglossal arch anteriorly and the palatopharyngeal arch posteriorly. They are a key feature of the oropharynx.

6. Relations of palatine tonsil all true except:

  • A. Anteriorly palatopharyngeal arch
  • B. Superiorly soft palate
  • C. Inferiorly posterior one third of the tongue
  • D. Medially cavity of oropharynx
  • E. Laterally superior constrictor muscle of the pharynx

Correct answer: A
a. (False) The palatine tonsil is located in the tonsillar fossa. Anterior to the tonsil is the palatoglossal arch (anterior pillar of the fauces), which contains the palatoglossus muscle. The palatopharyngeal arch (posterior pillar of the fauces), containing the palatopharyngeus muscle, is located posteriorly to the tonsil. Therefore, this statement is incorrect.
b. (True) The soft palate forms the superior boundary of the tonsillar fossa and thus is superior to the palatine tonsil.
c. (True) The posterior one-third of the tongue (base of the tongue) lies inferior to the palatine tonsil.
d. (True) The medial surface of the palatine tonsil is free and faces the cavity of the oropharynx.
e. (True) The lateral aspect of the palatine tonsil, forming its bed, is related to the superior constrictor muscle of the pharynx, as well as the pharyngobasilar fascia.

Q7. Sensory nerve supply of larynx below the level of vocal cord is:

  • B. Internal branch of superior laryngeal nerve

Q32. The following structures considered in supraglottic areas except :

  • A. Vallecula
  • B. Aryepiglottic fold
  • C. False vocal cord
  • D. Ventricle

Correct answer: A
a. (Correct) The valleculae are depressions located between the base of the tongue and the anterior aspect of the epiglottis. They are anatomically part of the oropharynx, not the larynx itself, although they are in close proximity to the supraglottic larynx.
b. (Incorrect) The aryepiglottic folds are key structures forming the lateral boundaries of the supraglottic larynx.
c. (Incorrect) The false vocal cords (vestibular folds) are located within the supraglottic larynx, superior to the true vocal cords.
d. (Incorrect) The laryngeal ventricle (sinus of Morgagni) is the space between the false vocal cords (superiorly, part of supraglottis) and the true vocal cords (inferiorly, part of glottis). The supraglottis extends down to the ventricle, so the ventricle is intimately related to and often considered at the boundary or within the broader concept of supraglottic structures depending on precise definition, but the vallecula is definitively outside the larynx.

Q55. The functions of the larynx are :

  • A. Protection of lower airway
  • B. Phonation and speech
  • C. Fixation of the chest
  • D. Helps in promoting venous return
  • E. All of the above

Correct answer: E
a. (Correct) The larynx plays a crucial role in protecting the lower airway from aspiration through mechanisms like epiglottic closure and vocal cord adduction during swallowing.
b. (Correct) Phonation, the production of sound for speech, is a primary function of the larynx, achieved by the vibration of the vocal cords.
c. (Correct) Fixation of the chest (thoracic fixation) is achieved by closing the glottis (Valsalva maneuver), which increases intrathoracic pressure and stabilizes the thorax for effortful activities like lifting, coughing, or defecation.
d. (Correct) The larynx, by maintaining a patent airway during respiration, allows for normal breathing mechanics. During inspiration, the decrease in intrathoracic pressure (thoracic pump mechanism) helps promote venous return to the heart. While actions like Valsalva (involving laryngeal closure for chest fixation) impede venous return, the larynx's role in normal breathing supports this function.
e. (Correct) Since A, B, C, and D are all considered functions of the larynx (with D being understood in the context of its role in normal respiration), this option is the most appropriate.

Laryngeal trauma

Q18. Regarding laryngeal trauma, incorrect statement

  • A. It is rare
  • B. Adults have calcified cartilage prone to fracture
  • C. Usually isolated
  • D. May present with dysphonia and distress
  • E. Penetrating injuries have worse prognosis

Correct answer: C
a. Correct statement. Laryngeal trauma is relatively uncommon, representing less than 1% of all blunt trauma admissions.
b. Correct statement. In adults, laryngeal cartilages (especially the thyroid and cricoid) undergo progressive ossification with age, making them more brittle and susceptible to fracture upon impact compared to the more pliable cartilage in children.
c. Incorrect statement. Laryngeal trauma, particularly when significant, is often associated with other injuries to the head, neck, chest, or multisystem trauma, especially in high-energy impacts (e.g., motor vehicle accidents, assaults). It is not usually an isolated injury.
d. Correct statement. Common signs and symptoms of laryngeal trauma include dysphonia (hoarseness or voice change), odynophonia (painful speech), dyspnea (difficulty breathing), stridor, neck pain, tenderness, ecchymosis, subcutaneous emphysema, and hemoptysis. Respiratory distress can be a prominent feature.
e. Correct statement. Penetrating laryngeal injuries can directly sever vital structures, involve major blood vessels, the esophagus, or the spinal cord, and often carry a higher risk of immediate life-threatening complications and potentially worse overall prognosis compared to many blunt injuries, although both types can be severe.

Q24. Which is incorrect regarding penetrating laryngeal injury?

  • A. Always you should examine the wound by probing
  • B. May associate with esophageal trauma
  • C. May associate with severe bleeding due to injury jugular vein or carotid artery
  • D. Has high morbidity and fatality in comparison with blunt trauma
  • E. Sometime associated with surgical emphysema

Correct answer: A
a. Incorrect statement. Blind probing of penetrating neck wounds in an emergency setting or outside of a controlled surgical environment is contraindicated. It can dislodge clots leading to catastrophic hemorrhage, convert a contained injury into an uncontained one, or introduce infection. Wound exploration should be done under direct vision in an operating room.
b. Correct statement. The larynx and esophagus are in close anatomical proximity in the neck. A penetrating injury to the larynx has a significant chance of also injuring the esophagus.
c. Correct statement. The neck contains major vascular structures, including the carotid arteries and jugular veins. Penetrating injuries can easily damage these vessels, leading to severe, life-threatening hemorrhage.
d. Correct statement. Penetrating laryngeal injuries, due to the high likelihood of associated vascular, airway, and esophageal injuries, often have high morbidity (complications) and fatality rates. While severe blunt trauma can also be devastating, penetrating injuries often involve direct violation of critical structures.
e. Correct statement. If a penetrating injury breaches the larynx or trachea (air-containing structures), air can escape into the surrounding soft tissues, leading to subcutaneous or surgical emphysema.

5. Penetrating injuries can lead to, except:

  • A. Surgical emphysema
  • B. Hemoptysis
  • C. Airway obstruction due hematoma formation
  • D. Esophageal injury
  • E. Good prognosis

Correct answer: E
a. Can lead to. If the air-filled larynx or trachea is breached by a penetrating injury, air can leak into the surrounding subcutaneous tissues, causing surgical emphysema.
b. Can lead to. Injury to the larynx or trachea can cause bleeding into the airway, which may be coughed up as hemoptysis.
c. Can lead to. Bleeding from injured tissues or vessels in or around the larynx can lead to hematoma formation. A significant hematoma can compress and obstruct the airway.
d. Can lead to. The esophagus lies posterior to the larynx and trachea, making it vulnerable to associated injury in cases of penetrating neck trauma.
e. Cannot lead to (this is the "except"). Penetrating injuries to the larynx are serious and potentially life-threatening. They can result in significant morbidity, including permanent voice changes, chronic airway problems (e.g., stenosis), and complications from associated injuries. Therefore, a "good prognosis" is not an expected outcome without significant and appropriate medical and surgical intervention; even then, complications can arise.

  • B. Bacterial
  • C. Fungal
  • D. Trauma
  • E. Tumor
