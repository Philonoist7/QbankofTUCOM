1. A 55-year-old woman presents with a first episode of PMB (Postmenopausal Bleeding). Transvaginal ultrasound scan showed an endometrial thickness of 3.8 mm. What is the most appropriate management plan?

  • A. Book a repeat transvaginal scan in 6 months
  • B. Book for an out-patient hysteroscopy to look at endometrial cavity and obtain endometrial sample
  • C. Perform a dilatation and curettage
  • D. Perform an endometrial sampling to rule out endometrial cancer

Explanation: For postmenopausal bleeding (PMB), an endometrial thickness of ≤ 4 mm on transvaginal ultrasound has a high negative predictive value for endometrial cancer. With an endometrial thickness of 3.8 mm and a first episode of PMB, reassurance and observation with a plan for further investigation if bleeding recurs or persists is often appropriate. A repeat scan in 6 months is a reasonable conservative approach if the bleeding settles.

  • C. Stage IIIA
  • D. Stage IIIB
  • E. Stage IIIC

Explanation: FIGO Staging for Endometrial Cancer: Stage IIIA involves tumor invading the serosa of the corpus uteri and/or adnexa. Invasion of the uterine serosa corresponds to Stage IIIA.

  • c) Luteinizing hormone (LH)
  • d) Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH)

Explanation: a) Progesterone: Progesterone levels rise after ovulation, produced by the corpus luteum. They are not "more common" in this group in terms of development. (b) Nulliparous women: Nulliparity (never having given birth) is a recognized risk factor for developing uterine fibroids. Pregnancy seems to have a somewhat protective effect. (c) Multiparty (Multiparity): Parity is generally considered protective, not a risk factor. (d) After 35 years of age: The prevalence of fibroids increases with age throughout the reproductive years, peaking around perimenopause. So, women after 35 (and into their 40s) have a high prevalence. Nulliparity is a distinct risk factor category.

31. In the following which is the classical symptom of uterine fibroid:

  • A. Menorrhagia
  • B. Pain in lower abdomen
  • C. Dysmenorrhea
  • D. Purulent vaginal discharge

Explanation: While fibroids can cause various symptoms or be asymptomatic: (a) Menorrhagia (Heavy Menstrual Bleeding - HMB): This is the most common symptom associated with uterine fibroids, especially those that distort the uterine cavity (submucosal, intramural). (b) Pain in lower abdomen: Can occur, especially with large fibroids, pressure symptoms, or during degeneration (e.g., red degeneration). It can be chronic pelvic pain. (c) Dysmenorrhea (Painful menstruation): Also common, particularly with intramural or submucosal fibroids. (d) Purulent vaginal discharge: Not a typical symptom of fibroids themselves. It suggests infection. While pain and dysmenorrhea are common, heavy menstrual bleeding is often cited as the hallmark or most classical presenting symptom.

32. Dyspareunia is the following except:

  • A. Painful menstruation
  • B. Endometriosis
  • C. Pain during sexual act
  • D. Pelvic adhesions

Explanation: Dyspareunia is defined as painful sexual intercourse. (a) Painful menstruation: This is dysmenorrhea, not dyspareunia. (b) Endometriosis: A common cause of deep dyspareunia. (c) Pain during sexual act: This is the definition of dyspareunia. (d) Pelvic adhesions: Adhesions (e.g., from previous surgery, PID, endometriosis) can cause pain during intercourse by restricting organ mobility. The question asks what dyspareunia is, except for one option. Since (c) defines it, and (b) and (d) are causes, (a) is the unrelated term.

33. Endometriosis:

  • A. endometritis
  • B. Adenomyosis
  • C. Presence of functioing endometrium elsewhere in the body other than uterine mucosa
  • D. Endometrial malignancy

Explanation: (a) endometritis: Inflammation of the endometrium (uterine lining). (b) Adenomyosis: Presence of endometrial glands and stroma within the myometrium (uterine muscle). (c) Presence of functioning endometrium elsewhere in the body other than uterine mucosa: This is the definition of endometriosis. Ectopic endometrial tissue is found outside the uterine cavity. (d) Endometrial malignancy: Cancer of the uterine lining.

34. Common sites of endometriosis is-

  • A. Ovary
  • B. pelvic cavity
  • C. Abdominal scar after LSCS
  • D. All of the above

Explanation: Endometriosis can occur in various locations. (a) Ovary: The most common site, often forming endometriomas. (b) pelvic cavity: This is a general term for the location where endometriosis is most common (e.g., peritoneum of the cul-de-sac, uterosacral ligaments, bladder, bowel). (c) Abdominal scar after LSCS (Lower Segment Cesarean Section): Endometriosis can occur in surgical scars (e.g., C-section, episiotomy) due to iatrogenic implantation of endometrial cells. (d) All of the above: Since the ovaries, pelvic cavity, and abdominal scars are all known sites for endometriosis, this option is correct.

35. Drugs are used for treating endometriosis include:

  • A. Combined oral contraceptive pills
  • B. Progesterone
  • C. GNRH analogues
  • D. All of the above

Explanation: Medical management of endometriosis aims to suppress ovarian function and/or the activity of ectopic endometrial tissue. (a) Combined oral contraceptive pills (COCPs): Used continuously or cyclically to induce a pseudopregnancy state, leading to decidualization and atrophy of endometrial implants, and reducing pain. (b) Progesterone (Progestogens): E.g., medroxyprogesterone acetate, dienogest, norethisterone. They cause decidualization and atrophy of endometrial tissue and suppress ovulation. (c) GnRH analogues (agonists or antagonists): Create a hypoestrogenic state (medical menopause) by downregulating or blocking pituitary GnRH receptors, thus suppressing ovarian estrogen production and causing atrophy of implants. (d) All of the above: COCPs, progestogens, and GnRH analogues are all established hormonal treatments for endometriosis-associated pain. NSAIDs are used for pain relief, and other agents like aromatase inhibitors may be used in refractory cases.

36. In stage II cervical cancer:

  • A. Carcinoma strictly confined to the cervix
  • B. The carcinoma extends beyond the cervix but not to the pelvic wall
  • C. The carcinoma has extended to the pelvic wall and lymph node
  • D. The carcinoma has extended beyond the pelvis

Explanation (FIGO Staging for Cervical Cancer): Stage I: Carcinoma strictly confined to the cervix. Stage II: Carcinoma invades beyond the uterus, but not to the pelvic wall or to the lower third of the vagina. IIA: No parametrial invasion. (IIA1: ≤4cm, IIA2: >4cm). IIB: With parametrial invasion. Stage III: Carcinoma extends to the pelvic wall and/or involves lower third of vagina and/or causes hydronephrosis or non-functioning kidney and/or involves pelvic and/or para-aortic lymph nodes. Stage IV: Carcinoma has extended beyond the true pelvis or has involved (biopsy proven) the mucosa of the bladder or rectum. (a) Carcinoma strictly confined to the cervix: This is Stage I. (b) The carcinoma extends beyond the cervix but not to the pelvic wall: This correctly describes Stage II. (c) The carcinoma has extended to the pelvic wall and lymph node: Pelvic wall extension or lymph node involvement would make it Stage III. (d) The carcinoma has extended beyond the pelvis: This is Stage IV.

37. Staging of cervical cancer is primarily based on-

  • A. Severity of symptoms
  • B. Age of female
  • C. Examination under general anesthesia
  • D. All of the above

Explanation: Cervical cancer is unique among gynecological cancers in that it is clinically staged (according to FIGO guidelines), meaning the stage is determined by clinical examination and allowed investigations before definitive treatment begins. Surgical staging is not the primary method for initial staging, though surgical findings can refine prognostic information. (a) Severity of symptoms: Symptoms can correlate with stage but are not the primary basis for formal staging. (b) Age of female: Age is a demographic factor but not a staging criterion. (c) Examination under general anesthesia (EUA): Often performed as part of the clinical staging process to accurately assess tumor size, parametrial involvement, and vaginal extension, especially if a thorough bimanual exam is difficult in an awake patient. This is a component of clinical staging. Other components include colposcopy, biopsy, cystoscopy, proctoscopy, and imaging (e.g., chest X-ray, IVP, CT, MRI, PET-CT as allowed by FIGO for staging). (d) All of the above: Incorrect because symptoms and age are not direct staging criteria. The primary basis is a thorough clinical assessment, which may include EUA, biopsies, and specific imaging studies as defined by FIGO.

38. A 63 years old female presented with lower abdominal pain and mass and was diagnosed as having an ovarian tumor. The most common ovarian tumor in this lady is:

  • A. Epithelial tumor
  • B. Germ cell tumor
  • C. Sex cord tumor
  • D. Stromal tumor

Explanation: Ovarian tumors are classified based on their cell of origin. (a) Epithelial tumor: These are the most common type of ovarian tumors overall, and their incidence increases with age. In postmenopausal women (like a 63-year-old), epithelial ovarian cancers (e.g., serous, mucinous, endometrioid carcinomas) are the most common malignant ovarian tumors. Benign epithelial tumors (e.g., serous/mucinous cystadenomas) are also common. (b) Germ cell tumor: More common in younger women and adolescents (e.g., teratomas, dysgerminomas, yolk sac tumors). (c) Sex cord tumor: This is an incomplete term. "Sex cord-stromal tumors" (e.g., granulosa cell tumors, fibromas, thecomas, Sertoli-Leydig cell tumors) can occur at various ages, but epithelial tumors are more common overall in older women. (d) Stromal tumor: Part of the sex cord-stromal category. Given the age, epithelial tumors (both benign and malignant) are by far the most common type.

39. The primary mechanism of action oral contraceptive pills is:

  • A. is the prevention of ovulation.
  • B. Progestogen-positive feedback works at the hypothalamus to increase the pulse frequency of the gonadotropin-releasing hormone
  • C. They increase the mucus production of the cervix.
  • D. They increase the lining endometrium thickness.

Explanation: Combined oral contraceptive pills (COCPs) contain estrogen and a progestogen. (a) is the prevention of ovulation: Correct. The primary mechanism is the suppression of gonadotropin (FSH and LH) secretion from the pituitary gland, mainly through the negative feedback effect of the progestogen (and to a lesser extent, estrogen). This prevents follicular development and inhibits the LH surge, thereby preventing ovulation. (b) Progestogen-positive feedback works at the hypothalamus to increase the pulse frequency of the gonadotropin-releasing hormone: Incorrect. Progestogens in COCPs exert negative feedback, suppressing GnRH and gonadotropin release. Positive feedback (by estrogen) occurs mid-cycle to trigger the LH surge in a natural cycle, which COCPs aim to prevent. (c) They increase the mucus production of the cervix: Incorrect. COCPs, particularly the progestogen component, thicken cervical mucus, making it more hostile to sperm penetration. This is a secondary mechanism. They don't typically "increase" the quantity of fertile-type mucus. (d) They increase the lining endometrium thickness: Incorrect. COCPs generally cause the endometrium to become thinner, less glandular, and less receptive to implantation (decidualized and atrophic with prolonged use). This is another secondary mechanism.

40. The most common cause of rectovaginal fistula is:

  • A. Obstetrical cause
  • B. Radiation
  • C. Carcinoma
  • D. Endometriosis

Explanation: A rectovaginal fistula (RVF) is an abnormal connection between the rectum and the vagina. (a) Obstetrical cause: This is the most common cause worldwide, particularly in areas with limited access to obstetric care. Obstetric trauma, such as severe perineal lacerations (third- and fourth-degree tears) during vaginal delivery, or complications from prolonged/obstructed labor, can lead to RVF. (b) Radiation: Pelvic radiation therapy (e.g., for cervical, rectal, or anal cancer) can cause tissue damage and fistula formation, but it's less common than obstetric causes overall. (c) Carcinoma: Invasive cancers of the rectum, vagina, cervix, or anus can directly erode into adjacent structures and form a fistula. This is a serious but less frequent cause than obstetric trauma. (d) Endometriosis: Severe, deep infiltrating endometriosis involving the rectovaginal septum can rarely lead to fistula formation, but it's an uncommon cause. Inflammatory bowel disease (e.g., Crohn's disease) is another important non-obstetric cause. Given the options, obstetric trauma remains the most prevalent cause globally.
