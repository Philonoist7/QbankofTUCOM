Bowel Obstruction

1. 40 years old male presented with gradually increased abdominal pain, associated with abdominal distention, nausea, 2 times of vomiting and constipation. He had history of anterior abdominal wall hernial repair surgery before 2 years. The most likely diagnosis is:

  • A. Bowel perforation.
  • B. Cecal volvulus.
  • C. Intestinal obstruction.
  • D. Acute appendicitis.
  • E. Crohn's disease.

Correct answer: C
a. Bowel perforation: Incorrect. While a possible complication, the gradual onset and history point more directly to obstruction; perforation usually presents more acutely with signs of peritonitis.
b. Cecal volvulus: Incorrect. This is possible but less likely than obstruction from adhesions in a patient with previous abdominal surgery. Cecal volvulus is more common in older, institutionalized patients.
c. Intestinal obstruction: Correct. This is the most likely diagnosis given the symptoms (abdominal pain, distention, nausea, vomiting, constipation) and the history of previous abdominal surgery, which is a major risk factor for adhesions leading to obstruction.
d. Acute appendicitis: Incorrect. The symptom complex, particularly the significant distension and history of prior surgery leading to likely adhesions, makes appendicitis less probable as the primary cause.
e. Crohn's disease: Incorrect. Can cause obstruction due to strictures, but in this context with prior surgery, adhesion-related obstruction is more common.

  • C. Ultrasound is the first imaging modality for intussusceptions.

d. Presence of a rim of air under the LT hemi-diaphragm is the sign of bowel perforation: Incorrect. Free air under the diaphragm (pneumoperitoneum) is a sign of bowel perforation. It is more commonly seen under the right hemidiaphragm. This describes a complication, not a feature of obstruction itself.
