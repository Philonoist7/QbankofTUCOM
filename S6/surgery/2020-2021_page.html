Subject Questions

General Surgery Questions

1. Which of the following statements is FALSE with regard to necrotizing fasciitis?

  • A. This is a surgical emergency.
  • B. It is a mono-microbial infection.
  • C. Must of the patients have a history of trauma or previous infection.
  • D. Wide debridement of the involved tissues is advised.
  • E. It can rapidly progress to septic shock.

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: It is a life-threatening surgical emergency requiring immediate intervention.
b. Correct: Necrotizing fasciitis is typically a polymicrobial infection (caused by multiple bacteria), not monomicrobial.
c. Incorrect: Most cases start at a site of minor trauma or infection.
d. Incorrect: Aggressive and wide surgical debridement is the cornerstone of treatment.
e. Incorrect: The rapid release of toxins can quickly lead to septic shock.

2. Regarding extracellular fluid (ECF) all are true, except:

  • A. Total ECF volume represents 20 % of the body weight.
  • B. Potassium is the principal cation in the ECF compartment.
  • C. The plasma volume constitutes 5% of body weight.
  • D. The interstitial fluid equilibrates rapidly with the other body compartments.
  • E. Extracellular fluid differs from intracellular fluid in electrolytes composition.

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: This is true; ECF makes up about 20% of total body weight.
b. Correct: The principal cation in the ECF is Sodium (Na+). Potassium (K+) is the main intracellular cation.
c. Incorrect: This is true; plasma accounts for about 5% of body weight.
d. Incorrect: This is true; water and solutes move freely between plasma and interstitial fluid.
e. Incorrect: This is true; ECF is high in Na+ and Cl-, while intracellular fluid is high in K+.

3. A second-degree burn is characterized by which of the following?

  • A. Coagulative necrosis extending to subcutaneous fat
  • B. Pearly white appearance
  • C. Anaesthetic
  • D. Erythema and bullae formation
  • E. Requires immediate skin grafting

Correct answer: D
a. Incorrect: This describes a third-degree (full-thickness) burn.
b. Incorrect: This appearance is characteristic of a third-degree burn.
c. Incorrect: Anesthetic (no sensation) is a feature of third-degree burns; second-degree burns are very painful.
d. Correct: Redness (erythema) and blisters (bullae) are the classic signs of a second-degree burn.
e. Incorrect: Immediate skin grafting is typically required for third-degree burns.

4. Which of the following statements regarding abscesses is false?

  • A. Staphylococcus aureus is one of the most common causative organisms.
  • B. The abscess wall is not lined by epithelium.
  • C. Most wound-site abscesses occur before the patient is discharged from the hospital.
  • D. Antibiotics are indicated if there is evidence of cellulitis.
  • E. Actinomyces can cause a chronic abscess.

Correct answer: C
a. Incorrect: Staphylococcus aureus is the most common cause of skin abscesses.
b. Incorrect: An abscess is walled off by a pyogenic membrane, not a true epithelial lining.
c. Correct: Surgical site abscesses often manifest 5-7 days post-operatively, by which time many patients have been discharged.
d. Incorrect: Antibiotics are added to incision and drainage if significant cellulitis is present.
e. Incorrect: Actinomyces is a known cause of chronic, suppurative abscesses.

5. Regarding radiation therapy for tumors, which of the following statements is false?

  • A. Currently, high-energy radiation is delivered to tumors primarily with linear accelerators.
  • B. X-rays traverse the tissue, depositing the maximum dose at the surface, and thus involve the skin.
  • C. Gamma rays typically are produced by radioactive sources used in brachytherapy.
  • D. The dose of radiation absorbed correlates with the energy of the beam.
  • E. Radiation therapy is a precisely targeted form of gene therapy for cancer.

Correct answer: E
a. Incorrect: Linear accelerators are the standard equipment for modern radiotherapy.
b. Incorrect: Modern high-energy X-rays have a skin-sparing effect, depositing the maximum dose below the surface.
c. Incorrect: Brachytherapy involves placing radioactive sources that emit gamma rays inside or near a tumor.
d. Incorrect: The absorbed dose is directly related to the energy of the radiation beam.
e. Correct: Radiation therapy works by causing physical damage to DNA, whereas gene therapy involves modifying a person's genes.

6. The distinct steps of T3 and T4 synthesis are the following EXCEPT:

  • A. Trapping of inorganic iodine from the blood.
  • B. Oxidation of iodide to iodine
  • C. Binding of iodine with tyrosine to form iodotyrosines.
  • D. Coupling of mono-iodotyrosines and di-iodotyrosines to form T3 and T4.
  • E. No hormone remain free in the serum.

Correct answer: E
a. Incorrect: Iodide trapping is the first step in thyroid hormone synthesis.
b. Incorrect: Oxidation of iodide is a crucial step catalyzed by thyroid peroxidase.
c. Incorrect: The binding of iodine to tyrosine (organification) is a key step.
d. Incorrect: The coupling of iodotyrosines forms T3 and T4.
e. Correct: While over 99% of thyroid hormone is bound to proteins, a small, biologically active fraction remains free in the serum.

7. An 18-year-old presents with a well-circumscribed 2-cm mass in her right breast. The mass is painless and has a rubbery consistency and discrete borders. It appears to move freely through the breast tissue. What is the likely diagnosis?

  • A. Carcinoma
  • B. Cyst
  • C. Fibroadenoma
  • D. Cystosarcoma phyllodes
  • E. Intramammary lymph node

Correct answer: C
a. Incorrect: Carcinoma is rare in this age group and is typically hard and fixed.
b. Incorrect: A cyst is typically fluid-filled and tense, not rubbery.
c. Correct: This is the classic description of a fibroadenoma, the most common benign breast tumor in young women.
d. Incorrect: Phyllodes tumors are rare, tend to be larger, and occur in older women.
e. Incorrect: An intramammary lymph node is typically smaller and firmer.

8. What is TRUE of basal cell carcinoma?

  • A. It may show a flat ulcer.
  • B. It may metastasize to lymph nodes.
  • C. It may metastasize to remote skin areas.
  • D. It is found exclusively in the head and neck.
  • E. It is best treated by topical 5-FU.

Correct answer: A
a. Correct: The ulcerative type of Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC), known as a "rodent ulcer," is a common presentation.
b. Incorrect: Metastasis from BCC is extremely rare.
c. Incorrect: Remote metastasis from BCC is extremely rare.
d. Incorrect: While most common on the head and neck, BCC can occur anywhere on the body.
e. Incorrect: Surgical excision is the standard treatment; topical agents are reserved for superficial types.

9. Which of the following features of cancer of the lung indicates distant spread?

  • A. Hypercalcemia
  • B. Cushing-like syndrome
  • C. Gynecomastia
  • D. Syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone (SIADH)
  • E. Brachial plexus lesion (Pancoast's syndrome)

Correct answer: A
a. Correct: Hypercalcemia is a paraneoplastic syndrome, a systemic effect indicating advanced disease, not local invasion.
b. Incorrect: Cushing-like syndrome is also a paraneoplastic syndrome indicating systemic disease.
c. Incorrect: Gynecomastia can also be a paraneoplastic syndrome indicating systemic disease.
d. Incorrect: SIADH is also a paraneoplastic syndrome indicating systemic disease.
e. Incorrect: Brachial plexus lesion (Pancoast's syndrome) is a sign of direct local invasion, not distant spread.

10. Diagnosis of duodenal ulcer is best confirmed by which of the following?

  • A. Urea breath test
  • B. Serum gastrin levels
  • C. Barium meal examination
  • D. Upper endoscopy
  • E. Upper endoscopy and biopsy

Correct answer: D
a. Incorrect: A urea breath test confirms H. pylori infection, a common cause, but does not visualize the ulcer.
b. Incorrect: This test is for Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, a rare cause of ulcers.
c. Incorrect: A barium meal is an older, less accurate imaging technique.
d. Correct: Upper endoscopy is the gold standard because it allows direct visualization of the ulcer.
e. Incorrect: Biopsy is often performed to test for H. pylori, but the endoscopy itself diagnoses the ulcer.

11. The most common identifiable source of a pyogenic liver abscess is:

  • A. Seeding from the portal vein
  • B. The biliary tree
  • C. Hematogenous from endocarditis
  • D. Direct extension of a nearby focus
  • E. Inflammatory bowel disease

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Portal vein seeding is a less common cause in the modern era.
b. Correct: Infection originating in the biliary tree (e.g., ascending cholangitis) is currently the most common cause.
c. Incorrect: Seeding from the bloodstream via the hepatic artery is an uncommon cause.
d. Incorrect: Direct extension from an adjacent infection is a possible but less common cause.
e. Incorrect: Inflammatory bowel disease is a known but not the most common cause.

12. Splenectomy patients should be immunized against all the following organisms, EXCEPT:

  • A. Haemophilus influenzae
  • B. Meningococcus B
  • C. Meningococcus C
  • D. Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • E. None of the above

Correct answer: E
a. Incorrect: Vaccination against Haemophilus influenzae is recommended.
b. Incorrect: Vaccination against Meningococcus B is recommended.
c. Incorrect: Vaccination against Meningococcus C (as part of the quadrivalent vaccine) is recommended.
d. Incorrect: Vaccination against Streptococcus pneumoniae is critically important.
e. Correct: Immunization is recommended for all the listed organisms, so there is no exception.

13. Which of the following is true regarding bile and gallstones?

  • A. The primary bile acids are deoxycholic and lithocholic acid.
  • B. The primary phospholipid in bile is lecithin.
  • C. Cholecystectomy decreases bile salt secretion.
  • D. Brown pigmented gallstones are more likely to be found in the gallbladder versus the CBD.
  • E. Bile consists of an equal part bile salts, phospholipids, and cholesterol.

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: These are secondary bile acids; the primary ones are cholic acid and chenodeoxycholic acid.
b. Correct: Lecithin is the main phospholipid in bile and helps keep cholesterol dissolved.
c. Incorrect: Cholecystectomy removes the gallbladder (storage organ) but does not affect the liver's production of bile.
d. Incorrect: Brown pigment stones are typically associated with infection and stasis in the bile ducts (CBD).
e. Incorrect: Bile contains these components in an approximate 10:3:1 ratio (salts:phospholipids:cholesterol), not equal parts.

14. Which of the following is not a parameter to assess the severity of acute pancreatitis in either Ranson or Glasgow score?

  • A. Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) and aspartate transaminase (AST).
  • B. White cell count
  • C. Serum amylase
  • D. Serum calcium
  • E. Blood urea

Correct answer: C
a. Incorrect: LDH and AST are parameters in both scoring systems.
b. Incorrect: White cell count is a parameter in both scoring systems.
c. Correct: Serum amylase is used for diagnosis, but its level does not correlate with disease severity and is not part of these prognostic scores.
d. Incorrect: Serum calcium is a parameter in both scoring systems.
e. Incorrect: Blood urea is a parameter in both scoring systems.

15. Which of the following is false with regard to an enterocutaneous fistula?

  • A. The commonest cause is postoperative.
  • B. A high-output fistula is defined as one where there is > 1 L/day.
  • C. They should be thoroughly assessed with barium studies and CT scans.
  • D. They always need an operation to cure the problem.
  • E. Hypoproteinaemia and sepsis often accompany the condition.

Correct answer: D
a. Incorrect: About 75-85% of these fistulas occur after surgery.
b. Incorrect: A fistula with output >500 mL/day is considered high-output, so >1 L/day certainly qualifies.
c. Incorrect: Imaging like CT and fistulograms are crucial to define the fistula's anatomy.
d. Correct: A significant number of fistulas can close with conservative management (nutrition, sepsis control); they do not "always" require surgery.
e. Incorrect: Large fluid and protein losses lead to malnutrition, and the breach of the gut barrier leads to sepsis.

16. Which of the following findings is consistent with the diagnosis of ulcerative colitis?

  • A. The rectum is not involved.
  • B. The disease is confluent, there are no skip areas in the colon and the rectum is involved.
  • C. The full thickness of the bowel wall is involved.
  • D. Microscopic examination of the mucosa reveals normal cells without evidence of dysplasia.
  • E. The incidence of colorectal cancer is equal to that of the general population.

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Ulcerative colitis (UC) almost always starts in the rectum.
b. Correct: This is the classic description of UC: continuous inflammation with no "skip lesions," starting in the rectum.
c. Incorrect: UC is limited to the mucosa and submucosa; full-thickness inflammation is characteristic of Crohn's disease.
d. Incorrect: Microscopic examination would show inflammation and possibly dysplasia in long-standing cases.
e. Incorrect: Patients with UC have a significantly increased risk of colorectal cancer.

17. What is the most common cause small-bowel obstruction?

  • A. Adenocarcinoma
  • B. Adhesions
  • C. Crohn's disease
  • D. Ulcerative colitis
  • E. Gallstone ileus

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Primary small bowel cancer is a rare cause of obstruction.
b. Correct: Adhesions from previous abdominal surgery are the most common cause of small bowel obstruction.
c. Incorrect: Crohn's disease is a much less frequent cause than adhesions.
d. Incorrect: Ulcerative colitis is a disease of the colon and does not cause small bowel obstruction.
e. Incorrect: Gallstone ileus is a rare cause of obstruction.

18. What is the most common complication of Meckel's diverticulum among adults?

  • A. Bleeding
  • B. Perforation
  • C. Intestinal obstruction
  • D. Ulceration
  • E. Carcinoma

Correct answer: C
a. Incorrect: Bleeding is the most common complication in children.
b. Incorrect: Perforation is a less common complication than obstruction.
c. Correct: In adults, intestinal obstruction (due to volvulus or intussusception) is the most frequent complication.
d. Incorrect: Ulceration is the pathology that causes bleeding, not the most common clinical complication itself in adults.
e. Incorrect: Cancer within a Meckel's diverticulum is very rare.

19. Why does acute appendicitis in elderly patients and in children have a worse prognosis?

  • A. The appendix is retrocecal.
  • B. The appendix is in the preileal position.
  • C. The appendix is in the pelvic position.
  • D. The omentum and peritoneal cavity appear to be less efficient in localizing the disease in these age groups.
  • E. The appendix is longer in these age groups.

Correct answer: D
a. Incorrect: Anatomical position can alter symptoms but is not the primary reason for worse outcomes at age extremes.
b. Incorrect: Anatomical position is not the primary reason for worse outcomes.
c. Incorrect: Anatomical position is not the primary reason for worse outcomes.
d. Correct: In the very young, the omentum is underdeveloped, and in the elderly, it is less robust, leading to a failure to contain the infection and a higher rate of perforation.
e. Incorrect: Appendix length is not associated with worse prognosis in these age groups.

20. Which of the following statements about haemorrhoids is true?

  • A. Most haemorrhoids are congenital.
  • B. Pain is usual in uncomplicated grade 1 haemorrhoids.
  • C. Piles which remain permanently prolapsed are called grade 3 haemorrhoids.
  • D. Portal pyaemia is a rare but known complication of haemorrhoids.
  • E. Profuse haemorrhage is never seen.

Correct answer: D
a. Incorrect: Haemorrhoids are acquired, not congenital.
b. Incorrect: Uncomplicated internal haemorrhoids are typically painless.
c. Incorrect: Permanently prolapsed haemorrhoids that cannot be reduced are classified as Grade 4.
d. Correct: Although extremely rare, septic thrombophlebitis of the portal vein (portal pyaemia) is a recognized complication of anorectal sepsis.
e. Incorrect: While uncommon, haemorrhoids can cause profuse, life-threatening haemorrhage.
