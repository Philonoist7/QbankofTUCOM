Pediatrics Questions

1. A 6-year-old child presents with pallor and decreased urine output following a recent episode of bloody diarrhea. Urinalysis reveals hematuria and proteinuria. A peripheral blood smear shows the presence of schistocytes. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

  • A) Acute post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
  • B) Hemolytic uremic syndrome
  • C) IgA nephropathy
  • D) Nephrotic syndrome
  • E) Henoch-Schönlein purpura

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Presents with tea-colored urine and edema after infection, not bloody diarrhea.
b. Correct: HUS triad is microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes), thrombocytopenia, and AKI, often following bloody diarrhea (STEC).
c. Incorrect: Recurrent gross hematuria with URI; no schistocytes.
d. Incorrect: Massive proteinuria and edema, not hemolysis.
e. Incorrect: Palpable purpura and arthralgia, not the hemolytic picture seen here.

2. An 11-month-old child is brought in for a routine check-up. The child babbles, has a social smile, and passes objects from one hand to the other. However, the child is unable to sit independently or crawl. All other developmental parameters are within normal limits. Which of the following best describes this child’s condition?

  • A) Normal development for age
  • B) Fine motor delay
  • C) Social and language delay
  • D) Gross motor delay
  • E) Global developmental delay

Correct answer: D
a. Incorrect: An 11-month-old should sit alone (typically by 6-7 months).
b. Incorrect: Transferring objects is appropriate for this age.
c. Incorrect: Babbling and smiling are appropriate.
d. Correct: Inability to sit independently at 11 months indicates isolated gross motor delay.
e. Incorrect: Only one domain (gross motor) is affected.

3. A 7-year-old child presents with fatigue and pallor. Initial laboratory investigations including CBC and peripheral blood smear are inconclusive. ESR is markedly elevated. There are no signs of infection, joint pain, rash, or other systemic symptoms. What is the most appropriate next step in the evaluation of this patient?

  • A) Antinuclear antibody (ANA) test
  • B) Bone marrow aspiration
  • C) Chest X-ray
  • D) Abdominal ultrasound
  • E) Serum ferritin and iron studies

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Low yield without systemic signs like rash or arthritis.
b. Correct: Pallor, fatigue, and markedly elevated ESR raise concern for malignancy (Leukemia), requiring marrow evaluation even if the smear is inconclusive.
c. Incorrect: Less specific than marrow exam for this presentation.
d. Incorrect: Leukemia is more common than abdominal tumors in this presentation.
e. Incorrect: Iron deficiency does not typically cause markedly elevated ESR.

4. A newborn is noted to have bilateral cataracts on examination. On auscultation, a heart murmur is detected. Which of the following is the most likely cause of these findings?

  • A) Down syndrome
  • B) Congenital rubella syndrome
  • C) Toxoplasmosis infection
  • D) Congenital cytomegalovirus infection
  • E) Marfan syndrome

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Associated with AV canal defects, not typically cataracts.
b. Correct: Classic triad of cataracts, deafness, and congenital heart disease (PDA/PS).
c. Incorrect: Associated with chorioretinitis and intracranial calcifications.
d. Incorrect: Associated with hearing loss and periventricular calcifications.
e. Incorrect: Associated with lens subluxation later in life, not congenital cataracts.

5. A woman delivers a baby vaginally after prolonged rupture of membranes lasting 15–20 hours. The newborn appears clinically well. What is the most appropriate initial investigation to assess for potential neonatal infection?

  • A) Complete blood count (CBC)
  • B) Blood culture
  • C) Chest X-ray
  • D) C-reactive protein (CRP)
  • E) Urine culture

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: CBC can be nonspecific in the early neonatal period.
b. Correct: Blood culture is the gold standard diagnostic test to identify bacteremia if sepsis workup is initiated.
c. Incorrect: Only indicated if respiratory symptoms are present.
d. Incorrect: CRP often has a lag time.
e. Incorrect: UTIs are not a common cause of early-onset sepsis.

7. A 2-year-old child with acute diarrhea presents with dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes, decreased skin turgor, and is alert but thirsty. Which of the following is the most appropriate initial management?

  • A) Immediate intravenous fluids
  • B) Oral rehydration therapy (about 750 ml) with close monitoring
  • C) Keep NPO (nothing by mouth) and observe
  • D) Start antibiotics immediately
  • E) Give anti-diarrheal medication

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: IV fluids are for severe dehydration/shock.
b. Correct: Signs indicate moderate dehydration. ORT is the standard of care (50-100ml/kg).
c. Incorrect: NPO worsens dehydration.
d. Incorrect: Antibiotics are not routinely indicated for acute gastroenteritis.
e. Incorrect: Anti-diarrheals are contraindicated in children.

8. A 12-year-old child is diagnosed with beta thalassemia trait after routine blood tests reveal mild microcytic hypochromic anemia with normal iron studies. What is the most appropriate management for this condition?

  • A) Regular blood transfusions
  • B) Iron supplementation
  • C) Folic acid supplementation
  • D) Regular monitoring without specific treatment
  • E) Splenectomy

Correct answer: D
a. Incorrect: Only for Thalassemia Major.
b. Incorrect: Contraindicated as it leads to iron overload in non-iron-deficient patients.
c. Incorrect: Not mandatory for asymptomatic carriers.
d. Correct: Beta thalassemia trait is a carrier state; usually asymptomatic and requires no treatment.
e. Incorrect: Reserved for severe hemolysis.

9. A 2-year-old child with severe malnutrition is brought to the emergency department. The child is lethargic, hypoglycemic on testing, and refuses to eat or drink anything orally. Vital signs are stable but the child appears weak. What is the most appropriate initial management to correct hypoglycemia and provide nutritional support?

  • A) Give oral glucose solution
  • B) Start intravenous dextrose infusion
  • C) Begin nasogastric tube feeding with dextrose-containing fluids
  • D) Administer intramuscular glucagon
  • E) Observe and wait for the child to eat spontaneously

Correct answer: C
a. Incorrect: Aspiration risk in a lethargic child.
b. Incorrect: IV fluids carry a high risk of heart failure in severe malnutrition unless shock is present.
c. Correct: NGT is the safest route to correct hypoglycemia and start refeeding in a lethargic/refusing child who is not in shock.
d. Incorrect: Ineffective due to glycogen depletion.

10. Poliovirus primarily affects which part of the nervous system, leading to the characteristic flaccid paralysis seen in poliomyelitis?

  • A) Dorsal root ganglia
  • B) Anterior horn cells of the spinal cord
  • C) Cerebellar Purkinje cells
  • D) Corticospinal tract
  • E) Peripheral nerve myelin sheath

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Affects sensory processing.
b. Correct: Polio destroys lower motor neurons in the anterior horn, causing flaccid paralysis.
c. Incorrect: Causes ataxia.
d. Incorrect: Causes spasticity (UMN).
e. Incorrect: Involved in Guillain-Barré.

11. A newborn has a total serum bilirubin level of 7 mg/dL. The baby’s blood group is O negative, and the mother’s blood group is A positive. What is the most appropriate management at this point?

  • A) Immediate phototherapy
  • B) Exchange transfusion
  • C) Reassure and monitor bilirubin levels
  • D) Start intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG)
  • E) Administer anti-D immunoglobulin to the mother

Correct answer: C
Explanation: There is no ABO incompatibility (Mom A, Baby O) and no Rh incompatibility (Mom Rh+, Baby Rh-). A level of 7 mg/dL is physiologic. Reassurance is appropriate.

12. A child presents with fever, cough, conjunctivitis, and coryza. On oral examination, you notice small white spots on the buccal mucosa opposite the molars. A few days later, a maculopapular rash appears, starting on the face and spreading downwards. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • A) Rubella
  • B) Measles
  • C) Scarlet fever
  • D) Chickenpox
  • E) Kawasaki disease

Correct answer: B
a. Incorrect: Milder prodrome, suboccipital nodes.
b. Correct: The "3 Cs" (Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis) + Koplik spots + cephalocaudal rash are pathognomonic for Measles.
c. Incorrect: Sandpaper rash.
d. Incorrect: Vesicular rash.
e. Incorrect: Prolonged fever >5 days, non-purulent conjunctivitis.

13. A toddler suddenly chokes while eating peanuts and presents with decreased breath sounds on the right side. What would you expect to find on the chest X-ray?

  • A) Right lung hyperinflation
  • B) Right lung collapse (atelectasis)
  • C) Left lung hyperinflation
  • D) Bilateral pulmonary infiltrates
  • E) Normal chest X-ray

Correct answer: B
Explanation: Foreign body aspiration can cause check-valve obstruction (hyperinflation) or complete obstruction (atelectasis/collapse). The provided answer selects collapse, a definitive sign of complete airway obstruction.

14. A patient diagnosed with kala-azar (visceral leishmaniasis) starts receiving treatment. Which of the following is typically the earliest sign of clinical recovery?

  • A) Decrease in spleen size
  • B) Increase in appetite
  • C) Resolution of anemia
  • D) Fever subsides
  • E) Weight gain

Correct answer: D
Explanation: Fever is usually the first symptom to resolve (days), followed by appetite improvement, while splenomegaly takes weeks or months to regress.

15. A 9-month-old infant has a sudden onset of high fever lasting for 3 days. The fever abruptly resolves, and shortly after, a pink maculopapular rash appears, starting on the trunk and spreading to the limbs. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • A) Measles
  • B) Rubella
  • C) Roseola (Exanthem Subitum)
  • D) Scarlet fever
  • E) Chickenpox

Correct answer: C
a. Incorrect: Rash overlaps with fever.
c. Correct: Classic presentation of high fever followed by defervescence and immediate appearance of rash (Roseola/HHV-6).
d. Incorrect: Rash coincides with fever.

16. A 7-year-old child with post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis presents with hematuria, edema, headache, and blurred vision. Which of the following is the most important clinical parameter to assess immediately?

  • A) Urine output
  • B) Serum creatinine
  • C) Blood pressure and signs of hypertensive emergency
  • D) Serum electrolytes
  • E) C-reactive protein (CRP)

Correct answer: C
Explanation: Headache and blurred vision suggest hypertensive encephalopathy, a life-threatening complication of PSGN requiring immediate BP management.

17. A 2-year-old child has a brief generalized seizure lasting less than 5 minutes during a febrile illness. There is no focal neurological deficit, and the child recovers fully within minutes. What is the most appropriate management and advice for the parents?

  • B) Admit for long-term EEG monitoring
  • C) Reassure parents and explain the benign nature of simple febrile seizures
  • D) Perform brain MRI urgently
  • E) Start antibiotic therapy

Correct answer: C
Explanation: This describes a simple febrile seizure. It is benign, does not cause brain damage, and requires no specific workup or maintenance medication.

18. An unresponsive patient is found to have no breathing. You check the carotid pulse for 10 seconds and find no pulse. What is the next best step?

  • A) Deliver 5 rescue breaths before starting chest compressions
  • B) Start immediate chest compressions (CPR)
  • C) Call for emergency help and wait
  • D) Open the airway and observe for breathing
  • E) Administer oxygen via mask

Correct answer: B
Explanation: Per BLS guidelines (C-A-B), if there is no pulse and no breathing, immediate chest compressions are the priority.

19. A child presents with hemolytic anemia, splenomegaly, and a family history of similar symptoms. Peripheral blood smear shows spherocytes. The mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC) is 40 g/dL. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  • A) Hereditary spherocytosis
  • B) Sickle cell anemia
  • C) Thalassemia major
  • D) Autoimmune hemolytic anemia

Correct answer: A
a. Correct: High MCHC (>36) is specific for Hereditary Spherocytosis (due to cellular dehydration). Spherocytes and family history confirm the diagnosis.
d. Incorrect: Autoimmune HA can show spherocytes but MCHC is rarely this high.

20. A newborn screening reveals high TSH and low free T4 levels, but the infant is clinically normal and asymptomatic. What is the appropriate management?

  • A) Reassure and observe
  • B) Start levothyroxine therapy immediately
  • C) Repeat thyroid function tests in 1 month
  • D) Start iodine supplementation
  • E) Refer for thyroid ultrasound only

Correct answer: B
Explanation: Congenital hypothyroidism requires immediate treatment with Levothyroxine to prevent irreversible intellectual disability, even if the infant is asymptomatic.

21. A child with prolonged seizures has been treated with intravenous diazepam, but the seizures continue. What is the next best step?

  • A) Administer rectal diazepam
  • B) Give intravenous midazolam
  • C) Give intravenous phenobarbital or phenytoin
  • D) Wait and observe
  • E) Perform immediate intubation

Correct answer: c
Why not the other options?
A) Rectal diazepam → Used before IV access or at home, not after IV diazepam failure.
B) IV midazolam → Another benzodiazepine; less preferred after diazepam failure (same drug class).
D) Wait and observe → Dangerous; status epilepticus requires continuous active treatment.
E) Immediate intubation → Considered if airway is compromised, but not the next step in seizure control.

22. A 15-month-old child with no prior vaccinations presents to the clinic. Which of the following vaccines should not be given at this age?

  • A) Measles, Mumps, and Rubella (MMR) vaccine
  • B) Pentavalent vaccine (DPT, Hib, HepB)
  • C) Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV)
  • D) Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine
  • E) Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (PCV)

Correct answer: D
Explanation: BCG is typically given at birth. After 1 year of age, its efficacy is variable, and it is rarely given without prior tuberculin testing; in many schedules, it is omitted for catch-up >1 year.

23. A 4-month-old infant presents with cough, wheezing, nasal flaring, and increased work of breathing. On examination, oxygen saturation is borderline low. What is the most appropriate initial management?

  • A) Antibiotics and corticosteroids
  • B) Supportive care with oxygen and fluids
  • C) Bronchodilators and antivirals
  • D) Immediate intubation and mechanical ventilation
  • E) High-dose intravenous steroids

Correct answer: B
Explanation: Diagnosis is Bronchiolitis (viral). Guidelines recommend supportive care (O2, hydration). Steroids and bronchodilators are not routinely effective.

24. A 6-year-old child presents with high fever, flank pain, vomiting, and signs of a urinary tract infection. Urinalysis confirms infection. What is the most appropriate next step?

Explanation: Systemic toxicity (vomiting, high fever) in pyelonephritis requires admission for IV antibiotics and imaging (ultrasound) to rule out complications.

25. A 3-week-old exclusively breastfed infant presents with mild but persistent jaundice. The baby is otherwise thriving, feeding well, and shows no signs of illness. Bilirubin levels remain mildly elevated but stable. What is the best approach regarding scheduled vaccinations?

28. A child presents with edema, hypopigmented hair, and features of protein deficiency. The weight-for-age is approximately 50% of the expected value. What is the most likely diagnosis?

29. A patient with diabetes mellitus has been admitted 3-4 times with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and severe hypoglycemia episodes. The patient also has a history of anxiety and marked glycemic variability. What is the most likely diagnosis?

Correct answer: A
Explanation: "Brittle diabetes" refers to hard-to-control Type 1 DM with wide swings between DKA and hypoglycemia, often associated with psychosocial factors.

30. A 10 kg child presents with the following signs: unable to drink or drink poorly, very sunken eyes, and skin pinch that recoils slowly (more than 2 seconds). What is the most appropriate initial intravenous fluid management?
